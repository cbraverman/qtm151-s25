Introduction:

When we first approached this project, we considered focusing on sex workers as our at-risk population. However, because the data was primarily opinion-based, we found it challenging to determine whether responses to the right to work question referred to employment in the sex industry or in more traditional sectors. Additionally, we recognized that the stigma and bias surrounding sex work could significantly distort the data, making meaningful comparisons difficult. As a result, we shifted our focus to people experiencing homelessness. We then analyzed survey responses related to the right to work, the right to education, and the right to health, comparing data from the United States, Mexico, and the Democratic Republic of the Congocountries chosen for their differing GDP levels and social contexts. We were excited to explore how perceptions of rights vary across these countries and what insights the data would reveal.

Insight 1: Homeless People and the Right to Work

In[]:
import pandas as pd
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

df = pd.read_csv('/Users/charliebraverman/Downloads/HRMI_Data_Download_2024_release/HRMI_Data_Download_2024.6.20/csv files/people_at_risk.csv')
target_year = 2023
target_countries = ['United States', 'Mexico', 'DRC']
right_to_work_var = 'work_atrisk_prop27'
filtered_df = df[(df['year'] == target_year) & (df['country'].isin(target_countries))]
df_work = pd.DataFrame({'Country': target_countries,'Proportion': [filtered_df[filtered_df['country'] == country][right_to_work_var].values[0]for country in target_countries]})
plt.figure(figsize=(8, 5))
colors = ['indianred', 'darkseagreen', 'palegoldenrod']  
bars = plt.bar(df_work['Country'], df_work['Proportion'], color=colors)
plt.title("Proportion of Respondents Saying Homeless People Are At Risk of the Right to Work")
plt.ylabel("Proportion of respondents")
plt.ylim(0, 1)
for bar in bars:
    yval = bar.get_height()
    plt.text(bar.get_x() + bar.get_width() / 2, yval + 0.02, f"{yval:.2f}", ha='center')
plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()
This chart shows the proportion of human rights experts in 2023 who reported that homeless people are at risk of being denied the right to work in the United States, Mexico, and the Democratic Republic of the Congo (DRC). Through our insight, we can see that opinions vary based on country and the biases associated with them. Experts in the United States were more likely to say that homeless people face risks to their right to work compared to those in Mexico or the DRC. As a higher-income country, the United States may reflect different expectations. Employment is often closely tied to housing and formal documentation, which may influence how experts perceive barriers to work. In contrast, in lower-income countries such as Mexico and the DRC, people without housing may still access informal labor markets, allowing them to participate in work despite unstable living conditions. This touches on the idea that the perception of risk could mean better conditions or simply different standards depending on the country. When looking at lower-income countries such as the DRC or Mexico, experts may not view homelessness as a pressing rights issue. This is not because conditions are better but rather because the right to work itself may not be consistently guaranteed.

plt.ylabel("Proportion of respondents")
plt.ylim(0, 1)
for bar in bars:
    yval = bar.get_height()
    plt.text(bar.get_x() + bar.get_width() / 2, yval + 0.02, f"{yval:.2f}", ha='center')
plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()
This chart highlights how human rights experts across countries perceive the risk of homeless individuals being denied access to education, with particularly high concern in Mexico (75%) and the United States (71%). In Mexico, despite free public education, systemic barriers like lack of documentation, transportation, and stability prevent many homeless youth from attending school. In the U.S., structural challenges within the public school system, such as residency requirements and administrative hurdles, can similarly exclude unhoused students, despite federal protections like the McKinney-Vento Act. Meanwhile, the Democratic Republic of the Congo (37%) shows a lower perceived risk, likely because access to education is already limited across the population due to poverty, instability, and conflict, making homelessness just one of many challenges. These findings suggest that in countries with stronger educational infrastructure, homelessness is seen as a more acute rights violation, whereas in lower-access contexts, it's viewed as part of a broader struggle.

plt.ylabel("Proportion of respondents")
plt.ylim(0, 1)
for bar in bars:

This graph depicts the proportion of human rights experts who believe that homeless people are at risk of being denied access to healthcare. All three countries report relatively high levels of risk, but the U.S. again has the highest proportion. This may reflect systemic gaps in healthcare access for the uninsured or unhoused, despite being a high-income country. In Mexico and the DRC, homelessness may be seen as just one among many compounding barriers to healthcare, including poverty and geography. Its also possible that expectations of what "the right to health" includes are lower in those contexts, reducing perceived risk specifically for the homeless. Given that the DRC is a lower-income, developing country, one might expect that access to health would be even more uncertain there, especially for homeless individuals. In wealthier countries like the United States and Mexico, health care is seen as a right and a standard public good. Being excluded from it due to homelessness feels like a serious issue. On the other hand, in countries where many people (homeless or not) struggle to access health services, this exclusion might be seen as expected or "normal," reducing the perception of being at greater risk.

Conclusion:

In conclusion, the overarching ideas of risk perception based on geographical location varies among the three countries we chose to look at. In lower-income countries such as Mexico and the Democratic Republic of Congo, we saw that experts viewed important human rights such as right to work, education, and health as less pressing despite the obvious financial disparities between those countries and higher income countries, such as the United States. An additional factor taken into consideration were the structural differences between the countries that impacted the proportions. Certain issues are viewed as broad or acute depending on the governmental policies or leadership in charge. This touches on a point beyond income and wealth, and digs deeper into political polarization.
After working on this project and working extensively with the codebook and datasets, we came to realize that the materials had some shortcomings. The codebook did not talk about how the study chose their respondents, which leads to the possibility that responses could be biased. The formatting of the people_at_risk dataset was also hard to understand at first, as each right was separated into its own column and it was difficult to locate the data we were looking for. However, the dataset does touch on pertinent issues and social groups that are often underrepresented in research.
